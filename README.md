Brother Jim and Sister Joyce: Portrait of the Artist as Borderline Personality
Rituals of Cleansing and Transubstantiation in the Ithaca Chapter of Ulysses
James Joyce’s genius was fueled by a complex web of relationships that extended far beyond his immediate family and into the intellectual scaffolding of his literary life. Throughout his career, Joyce relied on a series of "others," or "hosts," to offer him thematic and stylistic direction. These relationships were deeply rooted in psychological dynamics reflective of borderline personality organization—a condition where an individual cycles through multiple hosts over a lifetime, leaving behind a trail of emotional and creative exhaustion.

However, this interpersonal destruction was not a byproduct of his art; it was its engine. The 20th century witnessed a light-speed advancement of technology, art, and knowledge, bringing to the surface the historical calamities that accompany uncharted growth. As the fictions of law and society struggled to meet the complex reality of human life, Joyce’s work emerged as the ultimate diagnostic tool for the modern condition. He mapped the human psyche before the clinical vocabularies for schizoaffective, autistic, and borderline conditions were fully codified. By examining Joyce’s reliance on his human hosts, and his eventual textual resolution of these dynamics in the Ithaca chapter of Ulysses, we can observe an artist methodically shattering the human vessels of his own life to build a sacred, enduring architecture out of the shards. Creating a suitable critical body for Joyce is therefore not merely an academic exercise; it is an iteration toward understanding the varied temperaments of the human mind, minimizing suffering, and finding equilibrium.

Part I: The Psychological Engine and the Shattering of Vessels

Joyce’s utilization of intellectual peers was a structural necessity rooted in the mechanics of Borderline Personality Organization (BPO). To understand the engine of Joyce’s creativity, one must move beyond the myth of the detached artist and examine the precise psychoanalytic functions his relationships served. In this framework, the individuals Joyce drew into his orbit—his "fraternal hosts"—operated as what psychoanalyst Heinz Kohut termed selfobjects.[^1]

For a high-functioning borderline subject navigating an era of unprecedented psychological complexity, figures such as his brother Stanislaus, the writer Italo Svevo, and the painter Frank Budgen were utilized to regulate internal instability and to mirror escalating aesthetic ambitions.[^2] To extract their intellectual capital and weave it into his fiction was not, in his reality, an act of betrayal; it was the mandatory utilization of his own extended mind to process the sheer volume of data he was capable of holding.

However, the borderline structure cannot sustain these extensions indefinitely. The mechanics of Joyce's serial estrangements align precisely with Melanie Klein’s concept of "splitting" and Otto Kernberg’s formulations on the failure of object constancy.[^3] This lack of emotional permanence was preemptively colored by a paranoid obsession with betrayal. As Joyce frequently revealed in his fixations on Charles Stewart Parnell and Judas Iscariot, he operated under the assumption of inevitable treachery, constantly scanning his hosts for the failure that would justify their disposal.[^4] When a host exhibited personal limitations, demanded reciprocity, or deviated from Joyce's immediate utility, they triggered a fundamental structural anxiety. Joyce's psyche would abruptly "split" the host, shifting them from an idealized intellectual reservoir into a devalued, persecutory obstacle.[^5] The inevitable discarding of these men was a psychological "fratricide," a necessary defensive maneuver required for Joyce to preserve his cohesive dominance.[^6]

This relentless cycle of consumption and destruction finds its most accurate conceptual mirror not in clinical manuals, but in the Lurianic Kabbalistic myth of Shevirat ha-Kelim—the Shattering of the Vessels.[^7] In this mystical tradition, the infinite divine light (Ohr) proved too intense for the finite structures (Kelim) designed to contain it, resulting in their catastrophic fragmentation. Joyce’s borderline intensity operated as this uncontainable light. His human hosts, much like the traditional boundaries of the nineteenth-century novel, served as the vessels.

This was literal shevirah (shattering). As he famously confessed to Frank Budgen, a day's exhaustive labor might yield only "two sentences." This was not because he lacked the vocabulary, but because he was ruthlessly shattering the traditional English sentence, breaking down the fictions of inherited language, and rearranging the shards to contain an exact, hyper-specific frequency of thought.[^8] The resulting relational and linguistic shattering left behind fragments of broken relationships and fractured syntax. Consequently, Joyce’s ultimate artistic project became the ongoing work of tikkun (repair)—the meticulous reconstitution of these shattered fragments within the closed, highly controlled liturgy of his texts.

Part II: The Textual Vessels: Trieste, Limerick, and the Tragic Engine

If the psychological necessity of Joyce’s life was the repeated shattering of his real-world fraternal hosts, the aesthetic necessity of Ulysses was the creation of indestructible textual substitutes. He required two perfectly calibrated vessels: one to embody his own chaotic, unresolved youth, and another to represent the integrated, enduring host he could never sustain in reality. He found the blueprint for these vessels in the historical crosscurrents of his exile, forging Stephen Dedalus and Leopold Bloom into a Mosaic dyad of wandering.[^9]

The construction of Leopold Bloom—the Hebraic vessel of "being"—is deeply rooted in Joyce’s transformative years in Trieste. In this polyglot port city, Joyce immersed himself in Jewish intellectual circles, cultivating a vital relationship with Italo Svevo.[^10] His time with Svevo served as his second, and perhaps most productive, breakwater—a period of immense intellectual gathering that simultaneously pushed him toward a psychological breakdown. Svevo functioned as the primary fraternal host of this era, offering Joyce an intimate view of secularized Jewish life. While the real-world relationship with Svevo followed the borderline pattern of eventual distancing, his intellectual capital was permanently transubstantiated into the character of Bloom.

Following this Triestine exhaustion, Joyce necessitated a new host in Switzerland: Frank Budgen. Where Svevo provided the cultural intellect, Budgen served as a spatial and material selfobject. Joyce utilized the painter's visual logic to ruthlessly test the "muscle and bone" of his text, ensuring his textual vessel could bear the weight and physical gravity of Bloom as an "all-round" character rather than a flat literary construct.[^11] After Budgen’s utility was exhausted, everything ultimately came apart in Paris.

This trajectory illuminates the central tragedy of Joyce's life, one that strikes at the very heart of the human dilemma. His life serves as the antithesis to the mid-century American tragedy—the Willy Loman paradigm, where the house is finally owned only when it is too late and too empty to matter.[^12] Joyce, conversely, achieved absolute literary supremacy and attained "everything" in Paris, arriving at a point where he no longer cared about proving himself to anyone but his own artistic standards. Yet, the tragedy is that the proof-maker cannot escape his practice.

The very borderline mechanisms of extraction, shattering, and enmeshment that allowed him to conquer the literary world cost him what he loved most: the stability of his family and the sanity of his daughter, Lucia. However, this was not a mere weakness of character or a descent into static, co-dependent rot, such as the aristocratic decay later captured by the Maysles brothers in Grey Gardens.[^13] Joyce’s overindulgence in his own pathology was tragic, but it was profoundly meaningful; it was the active, agonizing sacrifice required to generate his monuments. He was charting the limits of the human mind at the expense of his own peace.

To ground this high-stakes internal drama in the text, Joyce had to activate Bloom as a true wanderer. He achieved this by anchoring the novel in 1904, the year he fled Ireland, and crucially, the year of the Limerick pogrom.[^14] This rare, highly organized instance of anti-Semitism in Irish history provides the essential atmospheric pressure for the novel. By placing a secularized Jewish figure in a nationalist, Catholic, and quietly hostile Dublin, Joyce fundamentally isolates Bloom. Bloom becomes an internal exile, navigating the streets of Dublin as a symbolic Jerusalem, carrying the weight of the diaspora within the micro-climate of Irish nationalism.[^15]

In stark contrast stands Stephen Dedalus, the Greek vessel of "becoming." Stephen is the unfiltered reflection of the young Joyce, lost in intellectual arrogance and terrified of the Oedipal trap. He is a young Moses in the making, wandering the same desert but searching for a host—a father figure to provide the necessary structure for his genius, while simultaneously carrying the borderline, patricidal urge to destroy that very structure.[^16]

Part III: The Liturgy of Mending: Ithaca as Textual Tikkun

If Joyce’s lived reality was defined by the relentless shattering of his fraternal hosts, the Ithaca chapter represents his ultimate, controlled act of tikkun. Beneath the chapter’s cold, scientifically detached catechism format lies a highly choreographed sequence of secularized rituals.[^17] Here, Joyce utilizes Stephen and Bloom to enact a syncretic liturgy that fuses Jewish halachic tradition, Catholic sacrament, and Freudian resolution.

The structure of the chapter serves as the initiation of this ritual, functioning as a secularized Passover Seder. Ithaca is a hyper-extended Ma Nishtana (the Four Questions).[^18] By answering these rigid, structural questions, Bloom fulfills the paternal Jewish obligation to pass the narrative of survival to the next generation, anchoring Stephen's chaotic "becoming" into a historical chain of "being." The catechism is not a stylistic trick; it is the rigid psychological containment vessel required to manage the overwhelming data of their shared existence.

With the narrative foundation set, the physical ritual of purification begins. In the Jewish tradition of the mikvah, spiritual cleansing requires "living water." Joyce’s exhaustive tracing of the Dublin water system is a ritualistic mapping.[^19] By ensuring the water’s unbroken connection to its natural source, Joyce establishes the halachic validity of the space.

The ritual then shifts to internal consumption. The boiling of the water and preparation of the hot chocolate functions as a dual sacrament, echoing both the Jewish kiddush and the Catholic Eucharist.[^20] When Bloom and Stephen drink, it is an act of literal and symbolic transubstantiation. Before they can proceed, however, Bloom must confront his own shattered lineage. He unlocks a drawer to reveal his most sacred relics. If the house is a temple, this drawer is the Ark of the Covenant, containing the suicide note of his father—the shattered paternal law, reflecting the broken tablets of Moses.[^21]

This prepares Stephen for his approach to the "Holy of Holies": Molly Bloom's bedroom. As Joyce explicitly revealed to Budgen, Molly was conceptualized not as an intellectual entity, but as the unstoppable, turning earth itself—a space of pure, terrifying natural force devoid of Stephen's neuroses. In the Jewish Temple, the High Priest could not enter the Holy of Holies on Yom Kippur without the Ketoret (burning incense) to obscure the Divine Presence.[^22] In Joyce’s profane inversion, Bloom lights an "aromatic pastille" before they move toward this earthen force. Instead of the father destroying the son—the fratricidal violence Joyce enacted on his real-world hosts—Bloom generates the protective smoke necessary to shield Stephen from the lethal reality of the whore-mother.

The recurring riddle of the chapter—“Where was Moses when the lights went out?”—signals the final liturgical crossing.[^23] When the lights literally go out, Stephen does not succumb to the psychic collapse he experienced earlier in Nighttown. Instead, Bloom lights a candle to guide him out the backdoor, functioning simultaneously as the Paschal candle of the Catholic Easter Vigil and the Pillar of Fire leading the wandering Jew. The liturgy concludes in the dark garden with the crossing of their urine streams—a secularized bar mitzvah and a final expulsion of the transubstantiated fluid.[^24] The crossing of the waters signifies the completion of the tikkun.

Conclusion: The Artistic Vessel and the Legacy of Repair

Through the rigid, syncretic liturgy of Ithaca, Joyce achieved the object constancy in his fiction that he fundamentally lacked in his reality. Within the safety of the catechism, the destructive cycle is halted. The father figure does not commit fratricide; he generates protective smoke, guides the son through the dark, and shares in the ritual of purification. By orchestrating this fusion, Joyce completes the tikkun, binding the disparate fragments of his intellectual, cultural, and psychological heritage into a permanent structure.

Reading Joyce through the dual lenses of post-Kleinian object relations and Lurianic mysticism reveals an artist in desperate need of external regulation, methodically shattering the human and linguistic vessels of his own life in order to build an unassailable, sacred architecture out of the shards. His fractured relationships were the unavoidable cost of his psychological survival, but they were also the exact engine of his artistic brilliance. In charting this agonizing pursuit of equilibrium, Joyce created a critical body of work that continues to iterate toward a profound understanding of human suffering and the diverse temperaments of the modern mind.

Pedagogical and Critical Footnotes

[^1]: Kohut and the "Selfobject": Heinz Kohut, a pioneer in self-psychology, defined a "selfobject" not as a separate person, but as someone an individual uses to maintain their own sense of identity and self-esteem. For a fragile or highly volatile psyche, a selfobject acts like a psychological prosthetic limb. (See Kohut, The Analysis of the Self, New York: International Universities Press, 1971).
[^2]: The Fraternal Host: For a detailed biographical account of Joyce's utilization and eventual discarding of his brother Stanislaus, see Richard Ellmann, James Joyce (New York: Oxford University Press, 1959), 133-137.
[^3]: Splitting and Object Constancy: In psychoanalysis, "splitting" is a defense mechanism where a person views others in absolute terms—either entirely good or entirely bad—because they cannot handle the anxiety of a complex, flawed human being. "Object constancy" is the healthy ability to maintain a positive emotional bond with someone even when you are angry with them. Borderline individuals often lack this constancy, leading to abrupt, permanent cut-offs in relationships. (See Melanie Klein, "Notes on Some Schizoid Mechanisms," 1946; and Otto F. Kernberg, Borderline Conditions and Pathological Narcissism, 1975).
[^4]: The Paranoia of Betrayal: Jean-Michel Rabaté notes Joyce's tendency toward "paranoiac" severing of ties and his intense obsessions with historical figures who were betrayed by their peers in James Joyce and the Politics of Egoism (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2001).
[^5]: Patricide/Fratricide: Sheldon Brivic explores Joyce's deep-seated psychological requirement to dismantle and destroy paternal and fraternal figures in order to assert his own dominance in Joyce between Freud and Jung (Port Washington, NY: Kennikat Press, 1980).
[^6]: The Mosaic Dyad: Ira B. Nadel outlines how Joyce utilized Jewish cultural and historical texts to formulate his characters in Joyce and the Jews: Culture and Texts (Iowa City: University of Iowa Press, 1989).
[^7]: Lurianic Kabbalah (Shevirah and Tikkun): A school of Jewish mysticism that posits the universe was created when divine light (Ohr) shattered the vessels (Kelim) meant to hold it, scattering broken shards across reality (Shevirah). The ultimate goal of humanity is to gather and repair these shards (Tikkun Olam). (See Gershom Scholem, Major Trends in Jewish Mysticism, New York: Schocken Books, 1941).
[^8]: The Two Sentences: Frank Budgen, James Joyce and the Making of 'Ulysses' (London: Grayson & Grayson, 1934). Budgen records the famous "two sentences" anecdote, illustrating Joyce's obsessive rearrangement of syntax as a literal shattering of standard English.
[^9]: The Construction of Jewish Identity: Neil R. Davison explores how Joyce manufactured Bloom's specific cultural background in James Joyce, Ulysses, and the Construction of Jewish Identity (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1996).
[^10]: Trieste and Svevo: John McCourt heavily documents the influence of Trieste's Jewish community and Italo Svevo on Joyce's worldview in The Years of Bloom: James Joyce in Trieste, 1904-1915 (Dublin: Lilliput Press, 2000).
[^11]: The Painterly Logic: Budgen serves as the primary record for Joyce's reliance on spatial and painterly logic to give Bloom physical gravity.
[^12]: Willy Loman: The protagonist of Arthur Miller’s Death of a Salesman (1949). Loman represents the tragedy of the American Dream—working a lifetime to pay off a house, only to achieve ownership on the day he dies, leaving it empty. Joyce's tragedy is the inverse: he achieved total ownership of his "house" (his literary supremacy) while alive, but the pursuit alienated everyone who might have lived in it.
[^13]: Grey Gardens (1975): A documentary by Albert and David Maysles depicting the reclusive, eccentric lives of "Big Edie" and "Little Edie" Beale in a decaying mansion. It is used here as a shorthand for static, co-dependent familial rot, contrasting with Joyce’s highly productive, forward-moving pathology.
[^14]: The Limerick Pogrom: In 1904, a Redemptorist priest incited an economic boycott and series of assaults against the small Jewish community in Limerick, Ireland. It remains one of the most significant anti-Semitic events in modern Irish history. (See Dermot Keogh, Jews in Twentieth-Century Ireland, Cork: Cork University Press, 1998).
[^15]: The Judaic Other: Marilyn Reizbaum, James Joyce's Judaic Other (Stanford: Stanford University Press, 1999).
[^16]: Stephen Dedalus: Hugh Kenner, Ulysses (London: George Allen & Unwin, 1980), 130-135.
[^17]: The Design of Ithaca: A. Walton Litz, The Art of James Joyce: Method and Design in Ulysses and Finnegans Wake (London: Oxford University Press, 1961).
[^18]: The Ma Nishtana: The Four Questions asked by the youngest child at the Passover Seder to prompt the telling of the Exodus story. Don Gifford and Robert J. Seidman explicitly link the catechism of Ithaca to this ritual in Ulysses Annotated (Berkeley: University of California Press, 1988, p. 566).
[^19]: The Dublin Water System: Gifford and Seidman detail the Roundwood reservoir aqueduct system exactly as Joyce maps it in the text.
[^20]: The Eucharist: Harry Blamires notes the Eucharistic echoes of the cocoa drinking in The New Bloomsday Book: A Guide Through Ulysses (London: Routledge, 1996, p. 205).
[^21]: The Ark of the Covenant: Harold Bloom discusses the thematic weight of Bloom's relics in James Joyce's Ulysses (New York: Chelsea House, 1987).
[^22]: Yom Kippur and the Ketoret: In the ancient Jewish Temple, the High Priest burned specific incense (Ketoret) before entering the innermost sanctum to create a cloud of smoke, shielding himself from the lethal intensity of the divine presence. (For halachic descriptions, see Philip Birnbaum, High Holyday Prayer Book, New York: Hebrew Publishing Co., 1951).
[^23]: Where Was Moses: Gifford and Seidman explicitly catalog the joke regarding Moses in the dark/sauerkraut in Ulysses Annotated (p. 599).
[^24]: The Crossing of the Waters: Richard Ellmann, Ulysses on the Liffey (New York: Oxford University Press, 1972), 160-162.
